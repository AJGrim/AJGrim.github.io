A Little Bit About Me

I'm a mechanical engineer with a strong focus on fluid mechanics and heat transfer, and a particular interest in systems where modeling and mechanical analysis must be done. I earned my Bachelor of Science in Mechanical Engineering in 2024 and my Master of Science in Mechanical Engineering in 2025 from the University of Delaware, with a concentration in aerospace and aerodynamic analysis.

My engineering process is rooted in the fundamentals: understanding a problem and the intended purpose first, careful brainstorming and modeling, and validating results through testing whenever possible. I enjoy problems that require moving between theory, simulation, and experiment, whether that means characterizing flow behavior, quantifying pressure losses in complex systems, or designing and testing thermal management solutions under real constraints.

Throughout my academic and independent work, I've pursued projects that bridge the process of analysis and hands-on implementation. These have included flow characterization and pressure-loss studies, thermal experiments focused on heat transfer efficiency and reliability, and simulation driven designs that were later evaluated through physical designs. I place a high value on clearly defined assumptions and unknown variables, clean organization of documentation, and designs that hold up when theory meets reality.

I'm especially drawn to engineering challenges where efficiency, reliability, and performance are critical; particularly in aerospace and high-performance mechanical systems. I enjoy tackling problems that are not fully defined at the outset and refining them into testable, defensible solutions through careful analysis and iteration.

About

I grew up taking things apart to understand how they worked — engines, electronics, anything mechanical. That curiosity is what pulled me toward engineering, and it's still what drives how I approach problems today. I'm most at home when a challenge doesn't come with a clean answer and I have to build toward one through analysis and iteration.

Outside of work I tend to stay hands-on. I follow aerospace closely — both the engineering and the broader industry — and I'm genuinely interested in where propulsion and high-performance systems are heading. When I'm not behind a desk or in a lab, I'm usually working on something physical, whether that's a project in the garage or just getting outdoors.

I'm looking for roles where the problems are technically serious and the team cares about getting the details right. If that sounds like where you work, I'd like to hear from you.
